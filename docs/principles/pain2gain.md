# Pain2Gain — understand a pain deeply enough that the gain is transformative

> Money is the world saying *thank you* for solving a real problem ([mission](../vision/mission.md)).
> The size of the thank-you tracks the **depth** at which you understood the pain. A band-aid on a
> symptom earns a band-aid's thanks. Heal the root and you change a life — and that is a gain worth
> building a venture on.

This is the **rigorous front end** of the [Builder Loop](../builder-loop/) — the discipline you run
in the *Understand* phase before you build. It's the companion to the
[5W1H critical-thinking grid](../curriculum/critical-thinking/): **5W1H scopes the pain; Pain2Gain
goes to its root.**

> **Not chasing comprehensive — chasing deep and specific.** You don't map *every* angle of every
> pain. You *discern* which dimensions are live for *this* pain, then drill those to the root.

---

## Where we are today (the honest assessment)

Before this doc, our understanding of a pain was **surface-level**: a single pass of the 5W1H
flashlight + "talk to three people, don't over-plan." Useful for starting fast — but it lands at
**rung 1–2** of the ladder below (a story, maybe a rough count). It does **not** reach causal
chains, mechanisms, distribution, or first principles. Pain2Gain is how we climb from *"we noticed
a problem"* to *"we understand exactly how this pain is produced, for whom, how badly, and why it
will keep coming back unless we change one specific thing."*

## 1. The Depth Ladder — how well do we actually understand this pain?

<p align="center"><img src="../assets/pain-depth-ladder.svg" alt="The Pain2Gain depth ladder, six rungs from shallowest to deepest understanding of a pain: 1 Anecdote (one real person's story), 2 Pattern/Survey (how common, how often, how bad), 3 Correlation (what makes it better or worse), 4 Causal chain (a why-chain from trigger to pain, evidence at each link), 5 Mechanism (the pathway through which it causes harm), 6 First principle (the irreducible root that would regenerate the pain regardless of any surface fix). Don't build before rung 4; don't claim transformative before rung 5–6." width="100%"></p>

| # | Level | The one-line test — *"are we here yet?"* |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Anecdote** | Can I name one real person living with this pain and tell their story? |
| 2 | **Pattern / survey** | Do I know what % of my people have it, how often, how severely? |
| 3 | **Correlation** | Do I know what makes it better or worse? |
| 4 | **Causal chain** | Can I draw a complete *why-chain* from trigger to pain, with **evidence at each link**? |
| 5 | **Mechanism** | Do I know the *specific pathway* through which it produces harm? |
| 6 | **First principle** | Have I found the **irreducible root** — the need, constraint, or paradigm that would *regenerate* this pain regardless of any surface fix? |

> **The rules:** don't build a solution before **rung 4**. Don't *claim* it's transformative before
> **rung 5–6.** The **first-principle test:** *"Would this pain still exist if every current
> workaround worked perfectly?"* If yes, you're staring at a symptom, not the root.
> *(Levels: descriptive → correlational → causal → mechanistic — Pearl's causal hierarchy; first
> principles — Aristotle, via Feynman/Musk.)*

## 2. The six dimensions — *which sides of this pain are live?*

Every pain has sides. Medicine learned this the hard way: the **biopsychosocial model** (Engel,
1977) replaced "it's just biology," and the **biopsychosocial-spiritual** extension (Sulmasy, 2002)
added meaning, because a person is a *being-in-relationship*. Translated for any human pain:

| Dimension | What to ask |
|---|---|
| 🔧 **Physical / operational** | What physical thing, resource, or constraint drives it? |
| 🧠 **Psychological / cognitive** | What beliefs, fears, or thought-patterns sustain it? |
| 🔁 **Behavioral** | What habits or missing skills keep it going? |
| 🤝 **Relational / social** | What between-people dynamics reinforce it? |
| 🏛️ **Organizational / systemic** | What structure, rule, or incentive *produces* it? |
| ✝️ **Meaning / spiritual** | What loss of purpose, identity, or dignity is at stake? |

> Discern the **2–3 live ones** for *this* pain. A pain that looks "operational" (no umbrella) is
> often really relational (a parent's worry for a child) or spiritual (dignity in the rain). The
> live dimension is usually where the transformative solution hides.

## 3. Get to the root — 5W1H → 5-Why (+ branches)

**Sequence matters** (Ohno, who built the Toyota Production System): **5W1H defines the problem;
5-Why finds its cause.** Don't drill before you've scoped.

1. **Scope with 5W1H** — Who has it · What exactly · Where · When/what triggers it · **Why it
   matters** (the stakes) · How it shows up. (See [critical-thinking](../curriculum/critical-thinking/).)
2. **Drill with 5-Why** — ask "why?" down each cause until you hit something you'd actually change.
3. **Force branches** so you don't tunnel on one thread. Two proven guards:
   - **Three-Legged 5-Why** — run *three* parallel why-chains:
     **Occurrence** ("why did it happen?") · **Detection** ("why didn't anyone catch it?") ·
     **Systemic** ("why does the situation *allow* it?").
   - **Fishbone (Ishikawa, 1960s)** — branch causes into categories (people · method · materials ·
     environment · systems …) so multiple roots surface, not one.

> **5-Why pitfalls (and the fix):** it can tunnel on a single thread, chase the cause you already
> suspect, and stop at the edge of your own knowledge. Fix: the three legs above, a **diverse**
> group asking, and **evidence at every link** (something you observed — not a guess).

---

## 4. Map the distribution — the "epidemiology" of the pain

A pain you can't size, you can't prioritize. Borrow from public health (WHO Global Burden of
Disease / **DALY**):

> **Burden ≈ Prevalence × Severity × Duration.** Frequent-but-mild and severe-but-rare both lose to
> frequent-and-severe-and-chronic. Build for the **high-burden** pains.

Also map the **shape**: **spatial** (which people, places, life-stages?) and **temporal** (growing
or shrinking? seasonal? spikes at a life event?).

> **Then add one vivid life.** Numbers tell you *how much*; a single **thick-description** story
> (Geertz, 1973) — one real person, what happened, what it *felt* like — tells you *how it works*.
> **N=1 deep beats N=100 survey for understanding** (a 90-minute interview reveals the mechanism;
> the survey only counts it). Use deep stories to find the right questions, then surveys to size it.

---

## 5. Ground it in a real need — what do they *actually* need?

People ask for faster horses. Look past the request to the need:

- **Jobs-to-be-Done** (Christensen; Ulwick): people "hire" a solution to make progress on a **job**
  with **functional**, **emotional**, and **social** layers. The pain is what blocks or degrades the
  job.
- **Self-Determination Theory** (Deci & Ryan, 2000 — strong cross-cultural evidence): is the pain
  thwarting **autonomy**, **competence**, or **relatedness**? This often reveals the emotional side a
  functional analysis misses.
- **Max-Neef's fundamental human needs** (1991): subsistence, protection, affection, understanding,
  participation, creation, recreation, identity, freedom. Watch for **"violators"** — fake fixes
  that *look* like help but block the real need. Aim for **synergic** solutions (meet several needs
  at once).
- **Kano** (1984): the sharpest pains are usually **broken "basics"** — things people take for
  granted until they fail.

---

## 6. Find the leverage — so the fix is transformative, not cosmetic

Donella Meadows' **leverage points** (2008) rank *where* to intervene in a system. Most people tweak
**parameters** (the weakest lever); real change lives at **rules, goals, and the paradigm/mindset**
that generate the system. If your solution only adjusts a number, you're treating a symptom. A
**transformative** solution changes a rule, a goal, or how people see the thing.

## The method, in seven steps

```text
1. DEFINE        — scope with 5W1H (who/what/where/when/why-it-matters/how)
2. DIMENSION     — which of the 6 sides are live? (physical · psych · behavioral · relational · org · spiritual)
3. DRILL         — Three-Legged 5-Why + fishbone; evidence at every link
4. DISTRIBUTE    — burden = prevalence × severity × duration; spatial + temporal; one vivid story
5. DEEPEN        — what rung are we on (1–6)? what one action climbs a rung?
6. NEED          — JTBD + which SDT/Max-Neef need is thwarted?
7. LEVERAGE      — Meadows: act at rules/goals/paradigm, not parameters → transformative
```

See the fillable **[Pain Dossier](pain-dossier.md)** — copy it per pain.

## Two gears: for the boys, and for ventures

**For the boys (kid version — keep it playful):**
- **Keep asking WHY** — at least 5 times before you stop. (The "Why Chain.")
- **Where does it hurt? When? How many people? How bad?**
- **What does this person *really* need** — not just what they asked for?
- **Draw the fish:** the problem is the fish's head; each bone is a reason it happens.

**For ventures (rigorous version):**
- **Don't prototype before rung 4** (a causal chain documented with evidence).
- **Burden score sets the priority** — highest-burden pain gets built first.
- **First-principle + Meadows check** before committing a solution path.

---

## The balance — depth without paralysis

Depth is the goal; *paralysis* is the failure mode, and it would betray the Builder Loop's
**fast · frequent · failing-forward** ethos. So:

- The dossier needs to be **"good enough to prototype" (rung 4)**, not "good enough to publish"
  (rung 6). Once the causal chain is solid, **two more interviews won't change it — go build.**
- Use Pain2Gain to aim *one* sharp shot, then let the [Builder Loop](../builder-loop/) iterate. Deep
  understanding and fast iteration are partners: understand the root, then learn the solution by
  shipping.

> **The 4-Question Test still rules** ([values](values.md)): is it true, is it legal & is my word
> kept, does it serve, would I be proud to explain it? A deep pain analysis that isn't *honest*
> about the people in it is worthless.
